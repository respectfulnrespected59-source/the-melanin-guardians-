# Melvinci — Guardian Prime

The first of the line to wake in this generation. The one the corridor recognized. Born Melvin Leroy Davis. Called Mello by his grandfather, his block, his people. Named Melvinci by the lineage itself — a name he hears for the first time in Scene 04 of Episode 1, though the ancestors have used it always.

He wakes in solitary inside Old Folsom State Prison. He is serving a sentence that should have been self-defense. The book is on his chest.

---

## The Three Names

The series operates on three registers of name. Each is true. Each is his. Which one the work uses in a given scene tells you whose mouth and whose world we are inside.

- **Melvin Leroy Davis.** The state name. What appears on the booking sheet, the indictment, the sentencing record. What guards say. What paperwork calls him. The name he was given at birth and the name the system uses to contain him.
- **Mello.** The block name. What East Oakland calls him. What Osanyin called him while he was alive. What his mother and his father still call him. What people who know him beyond the file use. Intimate register.
- **Melvinci.** The Corridor name. What the ancestors have always called him. Hidden from him until the moment of the revelation in Scene 04, when the lineage speaks the name and he completes the second syllable without hesitation — as if he has been hearing it in dreams for years and only now has the waking ear for it.

Transition rules: the cold open of Episode 1 deliberately gives us Melvinci's Corridor register first, before the audience knows he has any other name. The waking world of Episode 2 introduces Melvin Leroy Davis and Mello. The interplay between the three names is one of the series' primary storytelling instruments.

## Identity

- **Born:** East Oakland, California.
- **Age at Scene 01:** Late 20s.
- **Parents:** Mother, living, non-practitioner of the family's tradition, raised him with warmth. Father, living, see below.
- **Grandfather (paternal):** Osanyin. Died when Mello was 13. Raised Mello in large part before death.
- **Waking-world trade:** Welder and maintenance. Certified on arc, MIG, and stick. Learned the foundations from Osanyin fixing bikes and building small things; took it into adulthood and turned it into a trade. His hands know how to join metal and make it hold. This will matter.
- **Incarceration status at Scene 01:** Currently serving a 14-year sentence for homicide in **Old Folsom State Prison, Level 3**. At the time of the cold open and Episode 2, he is in **administrative segregation** — the hole. The conviction was wrong. See below.

## The Case

Melvin Leroy Davis killed a man in East Oakland who attacked him first. A single act of self-defense in a confrontation he did not start.

A Black woman — identity to be canonized in Episode 3 — witnessed the entire event and came forward to testify that he acted in self-defense. The court discredited her testimony because she was Black. Her statement was disqualified in effect if not in letter. The jury did not hear her clearly. The prosecution leaned on the absence of a white witness.

Melvin Leroy Davis was convicted and sentenced to 14 years under California's old-law 85% provision. He will serve the overwhelming majority of the sentence before he is eligible for release.

This is not backstory. This is the weight Melvinci carries into the Corridor. Every time he enters, he enters from a cell. Every time he returns, he returns to one. The ancestors know this. Osanyin knows this. The series does not flinch from it.

## Why He Is in the Hole at Scene 01 / Ep 2

Mello was put in administrative segregation following a yard incident he organized. The day before the incident, a younger brother from East Oakland was jumped near the back of the yard by the showers. Other Oakland men present did not intervene; they later claimed they were outnumbered.

Mello did not accept that explanation. East Oakland does not let its young get jumped while its elders watch. He coordinated a response through the underground cell-phone network active across the yard, gathered the soldiers, and the next day the record was set straight. The principle being enforced was not retaliation. It was **fair fights, or the yard gets torn up every time until fairness returns.**

He was identified as the organizer and placed in solitary.

This entire backstory is canon but is **not depicted in Episode 2.** Ep 2 stays inside the hole and the Corridor. The riot and its causes will be shown later — likely as a flashback in Ep 3 or beyond, when the antagonist arc requires the audience to understand who Mello is in the yard before he was who he is in the Corridor.

See `lore/concepts/oakland-code.md` for the full doctrine that informs this incident and Mello's ethics throughout the series.

## The Cell-Phone Network (Material Reality of Inside)

The yard at Old Folsom — and many California institutions — runs an underground cell-phone economy. Phones are smuggled in. Men use them to communicate with the outside, with each other across blocks, and with their families. **The phones feed people.** Money moves. Operators run businesses. Soft operators get their businesses taken — respect is material, not performative.

This network is how Mello organized the yard response. It will continue to be a texture and a tool throughout the series. It is not framed as criminality. It is framed as the survival economy of caged men keeping their families alive.

The series does not sensationalize this. It treats it the way the men inside treat it: a fact of the work.

## The Father

Melvinci's father is alive. He is a devout Christian — not a casual churchgoer, a deep one. His faith is his anchor and his cage at once. He is the son of Osanyin, which means he was raised inside the family practice before he chose a different road.

His flame is not gone. It is *suppressed*. There are sparks — moments when the old knowledge rises in him unbidden — that he pushes down because the doctrine he has committed his life to cannot hold the two things at once. He does not talk to Mello about Osanyin's practice. He does not tell Mello what the family line actually carries. He has a past Mello does not know about — a time before the conversion, when the flame was uncovered — and that past will come to the surface across Volume 1.

He is not the antagonist. He is the cost of survival in a country that punished Black spiritual practice into hiding for generations. Treat him with that gravity.

## The Awakening

Inside, Melvin Leroy Davis reads a book.

The book is **The Magnum Opus of Melanin** by Robert Hadden Jr. and Mahal Kita. It is a real book, authored by the real-world creators of the Melanin Guardians universe, and it exists as the same object inside the fiction.

The book does what it does. The knowledge of self arrives. Something in him begins to remember what was never his to be taught because it was always his to know. The subdermal light is already there, waiting. The Corridor has been waiting longer.

He closes the book. He closes his eyes. And the savanna of Scene 01 opens.

The cold open of Episode 1 (Scenes 01-05) is what happens on the other side of that closing of the eyes. When Episode 2 begins, we are with him again in the cell — specifically, in the hole — the book on his chest, glowing, the air in his chest different than it was before.

This is not a metaphor. The series canonizes the real book as the real catalyst. Anyone who watches the show and wants to follow Melvinci into the work can.

## The First Teaching (Episode 2)

When Mello returns to the Corridor in Beat 2 of Episode 2, Osanyin gives him **breathwork.**

Not generic breathwork. Specific:

- **Breath stacking** — controlled accumulation of breath to expand lung capacity and oxygen saturation
- **Wim Hof method** integration — rhythmic deep breathing followed by retention, used to access altered states
- **Meditation as portal** — the practice through which Mello can return to the astral plane / Corridor at will, on his own, without needing a book or a spontaneous crossing

The teaching's purpose is twofold:
1. **Communication.** Mello can now contact Osanyin for guidance whenever he can find five minutes of stillness.
2. **Activation.** The same breathwork harnesses Mello's inner chi / kundalini, which in this cosmology is the same energy as **full melanin activation.** The subdermal glow we have seen in the cold open is a baseline. The breathwork is the path to amplifying it into full power.

The breathwork is the foundation of every ability Melvinci will develop across the series. Vol. 1 is the practice of the breath. Later volumes are what becomes possible when the breath has been mastered.

## The Ritual Closing (ÀṢẸ)

At the end of Episode 2, after Osanyin gives Mello the teaching and the name, the two share a closing ritual exchange:

- Mello: *"And so it is."*
- Osanyin: *"And so it is."*
- Both, together: *"ÀṢẸ."*

Then Mello wakes.

This exchange becomes a recurring ritual structure across the series. Any time Mello and an ancestor close a meaningful exchange in the Corridor, this is the closing form. ÀṢẸ — pronounced *ah-SHAY* — is the Yoruba word for life force, divine authority, the word that makes things so. See `lore/concepts/ase.md` for full canonical handling.

## Appearance

- **Build:** Lean, strong, athletic but not bodybuilder-huge. Dancer-warrior proportions. Prison-sharpened — he has done the work inside.
- **Skin:** Deep umber, warm-toned. Never rendered washed-out. Specular highlights always warm gold, never white.
- **Head:** Shaved. No facial hair in Vol. 1 — he will grow a short beard by mid-Vol. 2.
- **Eyes:** Default dark brown. Amber-lit from within when melanin-activated. When in lineage-memory state, the ancestor corridor is reflected inside them (see Scene 04 for the lock).
- **Wardrobe in the Corridor (Vol. 1, Ep. 1):** Dark jeans, bare-chested, barefoot. No shoes in the corridor sequence. The clothing he wears into the Corridor is not his prison blues — it is something older, closer to what the lineage dresses him in.
- **Wardrobe in the waking world:** Prison-issued blues for the length of his incarceration arc. When the series moves past incarceration, regalia enters in its own time.
- **Subdermal glow:** Amber-gold tendrils tracing the veins, brightest at wrists, throat, sternum, temples. Intensity varies with state. See Scene 01 for the baseline look.

## Voice

- **Pitch:** Low baritone, unhurried.
- **Delivery:** Flat and true. He does not emote at his listener — he speaks as if each line is the act of deciding something. The prison yard taught him that most words are wasted.
- **Signature line:** *"I will never forget."* First spoken in Scene 04, recurring.
- **Ritual close (Ep 2 onward):** *"And so it is. ÀṢẸ."* (Said with Osanyin)
- **First voice direction for casting:** Think the gravity of a young Chadwick Boseman, the cadence of a young Mahershala Ali, the stillness of a young Lakeith Stanfield. The dignity of a young Yasiin Bey when he reads aloud. Not an imitation of any of them — that composite, specifically a composite that has spent years inside and has learned the economy of speech that inside teaches.

## Powers / Abilities

- **Melanin activation:** Subdermal light response to lineage states. First triggered in Scene 01. Path to full activation = breathwork (taught by Osanyin in Ep 2).
- **Lineage memory access:** Can enter and witness the Corridor of Lineage. Scene 02 is his first access. Each visit carries a cost we will define across Vol. 1. From Ep 2 onward, can access intentionally via breathwork.
- **Light-ripple recognition:** As demonstrated in Scene 03 — his presence triggers response in the ancestor line. This is not a weapon. It is a credential.
- **Breathwork (Ep 2 onward):** Stacking, retention, activation. The foundational technique.
- **What he cannot yet do:** Combat powers, flight, shielding. All that comes later. Vol. 1 is about awakening, not fighting. He is a Black man in the Old Folsom hole who has just started reading the right book and has just been taught how to breathe. That is enough for Volume 1.
